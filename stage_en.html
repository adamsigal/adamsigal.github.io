Duckietown

Duckietown (duckietown.org) is a robotics education, research, and outreach effort. The ambitious goal is to create the most broadly available autonomy platform in the world, initially designed by us, but eventually adopted and maintained by the community as a whole, perhaps with continued support to one particular institution for stewardship. We have carefully considered the balance of incentives to make sure that this work will have a life of its own, beyond our funding period. If this project is successful, we will improve the educational experience of all robotics students in the world. We will also save many PIs the time that they would use to create and maintain an engaging teaching experience. If, instead of developing this class from scratch, they now make use of our "class-in-a-box", then each PI can allocate more resources (intellectual / time / monetary) towards research. It is also a platform that can be used for cutting-edge research on topics such as: mobility-on-demand, machine learning / deep learning / reinforcement learning, resource-constraints, computer vision, and many others. At present, the platform has been officially used at MIT as a graduate class in 2016, and subsequently at Université de Montréal, Toyota Technological Institute of Chicago (TTIC), and ETH Zürich, for a graduate class in 2017. It is also unofficially used by many different institutions at many different levels (See duckietown.org for an up-to-date list).

The AI Driving Olympics and the goals of my project

Currently, the project is in a state that makes the barrier of entry too high for many people. A computer running GNU/Linux is required and there are many necessary configuration details. The objective of my project will be to learn the details of the robot's function (and consequently about self-driving vehicles) and then apply software engineering and robotics-specific techniques to make the platform more user-friendly and robust. The project is going to be used in a “live competition” at the prestigious Neural Information Processing Symposium (NIPS) in 2018 (we are referring to this as the AI Driving Olympics). There will be a series of challenges for participants to try. This will involve many students worldwide downloading the code and testing new algorithms. The teams with the best algorithms will be invited to participate in the live competition. My project will help to build the foundation for the AI Driving Olympics.
